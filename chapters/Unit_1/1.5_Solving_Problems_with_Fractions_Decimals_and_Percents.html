1.5 ‚Äì Solving Problems With Fractions, Decimals & Percents

Fractions, decimals, and percents all show parts of a whole ‚Äî but they‚Äôre used in different ways. We see them every day: in grades, prices, sales, surveys, and more.

This lesson will help you decide which form to use and how to solve real-world problems using these tools.

1.5.1 ‚Äì What‚Äôs the Part, Whole, or Percent?

In many real-world problems, you‚Äôre given some information and asked to find what‚Äôs missing. Maybe you know the total price and the discount, and you want to know how much money you saved. Or you know how many questions you got right on a quiz, and you want to figure out your percent score.

These types of problems usually involve three pieces:

  • part (how much you have),

  • whole (the total or full amount),

  • percent (the portion out of 100).

If you know two of them, you can figure out the third. This formula helps:

\[ \text{part} = \text{percent} \times \text{whole} \]

You can also rearrange it to solve for the percent or the whole. We‚Äôll walk through all three types of problems step by step.

Tip

Converting the percent to a decimal will make calculations easier!

Example 1 ‚Äì Finding the Part

What is 15% of 80?

You‚Äôre looking for a small part of 80 ‚Äî just 15 out of every 100. That‚Äôs what 15% means.

We can turn this into a math sentence by translating the words:

  • ‚Äúis‚Äù becomes =

  • ‚Äúof‚Äù becomes √ó

So:

what is 15% of 80?
? = \(\times\) 80

Now solve it:

\[ 0.15√ó80=12 \]

So 12 is 15% of 80.

üß† Try This: What is 10% of 50?


Turn it into a math sentence:

what is 10% of 50?
? = 0.10 √ó 50

Then multiply:

\[ 0.10 \times 50 = 5 \]

Answer: 5

Example 2 ‚Äì Finding the Percent

Out of 50 students, 30 say they like soccer. What percent is that?‚Äù

The phrase out of tells us that 50 is the whole and 30 is the part. We can write that as a fraction, then convert it into a percent.

\[ \frac{\text{part}}{\text{whole}} = \frac{30}{50} = 0.6 = 60\% \]

So 60% of students like soccer.

Tip

To find a percent, divide the part by the whole, then multiply by 100.

üß† Your turn: If 3 out of 100 students get enough sleep, what percent is that?


\[ \frac{3}{100} = 0.03 = 3\% \]

So only 3% of students get enough sleep!

Example 3 ‚Äì Finding the Whole

25% of a number is 10. What is the number?

We‚Äôre told that 10 is 25% of the total. That means 10 is just a piece ‚Äî one-fourth ‚Äî of the whole. (Since 25% = 1/4.)

If one-fourth of something is 10, then we can picture the whole as being made up of 4 equal parts of 10:

\[ 10 + 10 + 10 + 10 = 40 \]

So the whole is 40.

But what if it‚Äôs not so easy to think in fractions like 1/4? Here‚Äôs a trick:

25% as a decimal is 0.25. If we want to know,

‚Äú0.25 of what number gives me 10?‚Äù

we can work backward by dividing:

\[ 10 \div 0.25 = 40 \]

So the total is still 40 ‚Äî just another way to get the same answer.

üß† Your turn: 20% of a number is 12. What is the number?


Start by thinking:
20% = 1/5 ‚Üí So if one-fifth is 12, then the whole must be:

\[ 12 \times 5 = 60 \]

Or use division:
\[ 12 \div 0.2 = 60 \]

Answer: 60

1.5.2 ‚Äì Real-Life Examples

Grades

Mr. Ross hands you back your Algebra quiz. You got 19 out of 20 points! What is your grade?

Grades are given as percentages so we want to figure out what \(\frac{19}{20}\) is as a percent.

\[ \frac{19}{20} = 0.95 = 95\% \]

You got 95% on that assignment, that‚Äôs an A!

üß† If John got 20 out of 25 questions correct, what is his grade?

\[ \frac{20}{25} = 0.8 = 80\% \]

John got an 80% which is a B.

Sales & Discounts

A clothing store is having a back-to-school sale. A $60 jacket is on sale for 25% off. What‚Äôs the new price?
First, find the discount by asking ‚ÄúWhat is 25% of $60?‚Äù:

what is 25% of $60?
? = 0.25 √ó 60

\[ 0.25 \times 60 = 15 \]

This tells us we will save $15. To find the new price we subtract:

\[ 60 - 15 = 45 \]

The jacket now costs $45.

üß† Swanhilda finds a $300 prom dress on sale at 65% off. What does the dress cost?

Find the discount:

what is 65% of $300?
? = 0.65 √ó 300

\[ 0.65 \times 300 = 195 \]

Now subtract to get the sale price:

\[ 300 - 195 = 105 \]

Swanhilda pays $105 for the dress. What a steal!

Surveys & Data

A survey of teachers asked whether they prefer cookies or cake. The survey found that 75% prefer cookies. If 120 of the teachers prefered cookies, how many teachers were surveyed?

Here we want to know the whole when we have a percent. First, convert 75% to a decimal:

\[ 75\% = 0.75 \]

Then divide:

\[ 120 \div 0.75 = 160 \]

160 teachers were surveyed.

üß† A survey asked Missourians if they support Proposition A. 550 (or 55%) said they do support it. How many people were surveyed?


First, convert to a decimal:

\[ 55\% = 0.55 \]

Then divide:

\[ 550 \div 0.55 = 1000 \]

1000 Missourians were surveyed.
