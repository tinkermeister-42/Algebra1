1.2 - Factors, Multiples & Prime Factorization

Have you ever had to split something up evenly ‚Äî like slices of pizza or players on a team? That‚Äôs really what factors are about: dividing numbers into equal parts.

In this lesson, you‚Äôll learn how to:

  • Spot factors and multiples.

  • Tell if a number is prime number or composite number.

  • Break numbers into their basic building blocks using a factor tree.

You‚Äôll use these skills again and again ‚Äî from simplifying fractions to solving equations.

1.2.1 - What Are Factors?

A factor of a number is a whole number that divides it evenly ‚Äî with no remainder.

Example:

The factors of 12 are: 1, 2, 3, 4, 6 and 12

That‚Äôs because:

1 √ó 12 = 12

2 √ó 6 = 12

3 √ó 4 = 12

Only one number does: 1. It only has itself as a factor!

1.2.2 - What Are Multiples?

A multiple is what you get when you multiply a number by 1, 2, 3, 4‚Ä¶

Example:

Here are the first few multiples of 5:

\(5, 10, 15, 20, 25, 30, ...\)

Where have we seen this before?

Multiples show up all over the place. When you skip count, you are using multiples. In the previous lesson, we used multiples to construct number lines!

Example:

Here is a number line that shows multiples of three.

If a bus comes every 15 minutes, the arrival times are multiples of 15.

1.2.3 - Prime vs. Composite

Some numbers can only be divided evenly two ways ‚Äî others can be broken into many parts. Let‚Äôs explore the difference between prime numbers and composite numbers.

A prime number has only 2 factors: 1 and itself.

Examples: 2, 3, 5, 7, 11, 13‚Ä¶

A composite number has more than 2 factors.

Examples: 4, 6, 8, 9, 10‚Ä¶

People around the world spend years searching for giant prime numbers. The largest ones we‚Äôve found so far have over 24 million digits!

Why go to all that trouble?

Prime numbers are the secret behind modern encryption. They‚Äôre used to protect everything from bank accounts to private messages. The bigger the primes, the harder they are to break ‚Äî which is why finding huge ones is such a big deal.

It‚Äôs like a global math scavenger hunt‚Ä¶ with real-world consequences.

1.2.4 - Prime Factorization and Factor Trees

Every number can be broken into a product of prime numbers ‚Äî sort of like breaking a LEGO¬Æ sculpture into individual bricks. These prime factors are the basic building blocks of all whole numbers.

We use factor trees to find these prime factors. This isn‚Äôt just a fun trick ‚Äî it builds your number sense: your ability to see patterns, understand how numbers are structured, and work confidently with them.

That number sense will come in handy later when you:

  • Simplify fractions
  • Solve equations
  • Factor algebraic expressions

Let‚Äôs build a factor tree for 360 to see how it works.
