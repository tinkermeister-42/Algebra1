2.1 ‚Äì Expressions & Their Parts

Algebra is a language made of expressions ‚Äî math phrases that use numbers, symbols, and variables to describe patterns or situations. Before we learn to simplify or solve anything, we need to understand what expressions are and how to describe their parts.

In this lesson, you‚Äôll learn what a variable is, what makes up an expression, and how to recognize important parts like terms, coefficients, and constants.

coefficient, constant, expression, term, variable

2.1.1 ‚Äì What Is a Variable?

Sometimes we need to talk about a number we don‚Äôt know yet‚Äîor one that can change. For that, we use a variable.

A variable is a letter or symbol that stands for an unknown or changing number. You can use many letters, but in algebra we most often use x.

Think of an x as the label on a mystery box. You know the box holds a number inside, but you don‚Äôt know which number it is yet!

Example:

You work at a grocery store. Your boss hands you a stack of labels and points to some apple boxes: ‚ÄúWrite how many apples are in each box.‚Äù

You pick up a sealed box. You can‚Äôt see inside, so you write x on the label ‚Äî x stands for ‚Äúthe number of apples in this box (unknown).‚Äù

Your boss isn‚Äôt satisfied and says, ‚ÄúCheck inside.‚Äù You open the box and peek: there are 3 apples.

Now that you know the number, you change the label to 3.

Before you opened it, x was a variable‚Äîa placeholder for an unknown number. After you looked, you found \(x = 3\), so you replaced the variable with the number.

2.1.2 ‚Äì What Is an Expression?

An expression is a math phrase made of numbers, variables, and operations ‚Äî but no equal sign.

Examples (expressions):

  • \(3x + 5\)
  • \(x - 7\)
  • \(\tfrac{1}{2}(y + 4)\)
  • \(4(a - 3)\)

Not expressions (these make a claim):

  • \(3x + 5 = 20\) (has an equals sign ‚Üí an equation)
  • \(x > 7\) (comparison)

In algebra we often multiply by just putting things next to each other. We call this multiplication by proximity.

  • \(3x\) means \(3 \times x\)
  • \(4(a-3)\) means \(4 \times (a-3)\)

Price √ó quantity: If stickers cost $0.50 each and you buy \(s\) stickers, the cost is \(0.50s\). That‚Äôs multiplication by proximity in the wild.

Visual idea: Two columns ‚ÄúExpression / Not an Expression.‚Äù On the right, highlight the equals sign in color. On the left, show \(3x\) with a tiny label ‚Äúproximity = multiply.‚Äù

2.1.3 ‚Äì Terms, Coefficients, and Constants

Consider:

\[ 4x + 2y - 7 \]

  • Terms are the chunks separated by \(+\) or \(-\): \(4x\), \(2y\), \(-7\).
  • A coefficient tells ‚Äúhow many copies of the variable‚Äù: 4 is the coefficient of \(x\); 2 is the coefficient of \(y\).
  • A constant is a number that doesn‚Äôt change: \(-7\).
  • The sign in front belongs to the term ‚Üí the last term here is \(-7\).
  • A lone variable has an implied coefficient of 1. Examples: coefficient of \(x\) is \(1\); coefficient of \(-y\) is \(-1\).

Visual idea: Show \(4x + 2y - 7\). Circle each term, box the coefficients, underline the constant. Add a mini-example \(-y + 9\) to spotlight the coefficient \(-1\) idea.

Answer: terms: \(9\), \(-3x\), \(y\); coefficients: \(x \to -3\), \(y \to 1\); constant: \(9\).

2.1.4 ‚Äì Reading Expressions in Words

Expressions are short stories.

  • \(10h\): ‚Äú$10 per hour for \(h\) hours.‚Äù
  • \(s + 15\): ‚Äústart at speed \(s\), then increase by 15.‚Äù
  • \(5x\): ‚Äú5 in each group, \(x\) groups.‚Äù
  • \(2(x + 3)\): ‚Äúdouble the amount after adding 3.‚Äù
  • ‚ÄúTwice a number increased by 3‚Äù ‚Üí \(2x + 3\)
  • ‚ÄúTwice the number increased by 3‚Äù ‚Üí \(2(x + 3)\) Those are not the same.

Step then scale: A club charges a $5 sign-up fee and then doubles your donation. If you donate \(d\), the club adds $5 first, then doubles: \(2(d + 5)\).

Answer: ‚ÄúTake \(y\), subtract 2, then triple the result.‚Äù First subtract, then multiply.

2.1.5 ‚Äì Writing Expressions for Situations

From situation ‚Üí expression:

  • You have 3 more apples than your friend, who has \(x\). ‚Üí \(x + 3\)
  • A shirt costs $12. You buy \(n\) shirts. ‚Üí \(12n\) (multiplication by proximity)
  • A plant is 8 inches tall and grows \(g\) inches each day. ‚Üí \(8 + g\)
  • You spend $20 and then buy 2 items that cost \(x\) each. ‚Üí \(20 + 2x\)

Travel: A bus goes 45 miles per hour. After \(t\) hours, the distance is \(45t\). That‚Äôs proximity multiplication again (speed √ó time).

Answer: \(3 + 2m\).

Answer: width \(= L - 3\); perimeter \(= 2L + 2(L - 3) = 4L - 6\).

2.1.6 ‚Äì Concept Lab: Build an Expression

We‚Äôll build expressions from pieces to feel what each part means. (Tiles, paper strips, or quick sketches work great.)

  1. ‚ÄúThree \(x\)‚Äôs and two more.‚Äù Arrange \(x, x, x, 1, 1\). Write: \(3x + 2\).
  2. ‚ÄúDouble the amount after adding 4.‚Äù Build \(x, 1, 1, 1, 1\); then duplicate the group. Write: \(2(x+4)\).
  3. ‚ÄúStart at 10, then lose 2 each game for \(g\) games.‚Äù Write: \(10 - 2g\).

Visual idea: A photo/diagram of (a) \(3x+2\) arranged with tiles, (b) two identical groups for \(2(x+4)\). Minimal labels so students do the talking.

2.1.7 ‚Äì Common Misconceptions

  • Don‚Äôt add a ‚Äò√ó‚Äô: \(3x\) already means multiply (multiplication by proximity).
  • Sign goes with the term: in \(9 - 2x\), the second term is \(-2x\).
  • ‚ÄúLess than‚Äù flips order: ‚Äú6 fewer than a number‚Äù ‚Üí \(x - 6\), not \(6 - x\).
  • Coefficient ‚â† exponent: in \(4x^2\), 4 is the coefficient; the 2 is an exponent (‚Äúsquare‚Äù).
