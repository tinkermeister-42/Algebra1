2.1.1 ‚Äì What Is a Variable?

Numbers are very important in math. But sometimes, when trying to solve a problem or describe a situation, a way to talk about a number that is not known yet, or a number that can change, is needed. To do this, something special called a variable is used.

A variable is a letter or symbol used to represent an unknown or changing number. Any symbol can be used for a variable but, in Algebra, the most common symbol we will use is the letter x.

Think of a x as the label on a mystery box. You know the box holds a number inside, but you don‚Äôt know which number it is yet!

Let‚Äôs look at some examples:

Example: Imagine you have some apples, but you don‚Äôt know exactly how many. You can use the letter a as a placeholder for the number of apples you have. If you get 5 more apples, the number of apples you have now can be shown as: a + 5 Suggested Visual: Instead of: a number machine, which might be too abstract. Try: A simple drawing of a box with a question mark on it, and the letter ‚Äòa‚Äô written next to it. Then draw 5 more apples next to the box. This approach uses a concrete analogy (‚Äúmystery box‚Äù), avoids jargon initially, and immediately connects the concept to a tangible object (apples). It also sets up the next examples with a clear, relatable scenario. Remember to speak slowly and clearly when introducing these concepts. You can also invite students to offer other real-world examples of quantities that can change or be unknown.

2.1.2 ‚Äì What Is an Expression?

An expression is a math phrase that combines numbers, variables, and operations ‚Äî but no equal sign.

Examples of expressions: - (3x + 5)

These expressions tell us something ‚Äî like a rule, or a situation ‚Äî but they don‚Äôt make a claim about what the answer is.
